My process

  1. Design strategy

    Often times I see designers and project teams jumping into design and development without enough consideration for the design strategy. Before starting a project, I like to consider all stakeholders and encourage them to break out of their segmented thinking. If possible, I like to use design sprints and working sessions to collaborate and discuss the applications involved, the architecture and of course the needs of the end users. Strategy sessions help to generate conceptual designs and high level prototypes. Additionally, these sessions can highlight any gaps where we may need to conduct research and analysis.

  2. User-research & analysis

    User research & analysis is an extremely important input to my design process. Understanding a user's behavior and mental model has allowed me to create designs that address the user's actual needs and not just their perceived ones. Performing contextual interviews, observation, and competitor analysis are all effective methods of user research that I have used as inputs to my designs.

  3. Design & ideation

    Starting with pen and paper is always my preferred method when generating ideas for a complex workflow. While working on insurance applications, I often encounter complex business workflows. I find that writing out all the possible scenarios helps me to organize my thoughts and consider the workflow without fixating on a detailed solution too early. Once I have some conceptual sketches, I will work to develop some more detailed designs and prototypes. Peer review is extremely important for me, and I always look for feedback from my fellow designers and engineers. Once I have gained buy-in on a design, I will polish it up and prepare it to share with the engineer building the feature.

  4. Implementation

    Working with developers is crucial to being a good user experience designer, and my understanding of front-end development has been a great asset for me. Once designs have been finished, I will pass the specs over to the engineer, but the work doesn't stop there. Working with developers to implement the solution and make adjustments is critical. Things don't always go as planned, so making sure that the lines of communication are open with the development team is a must.

  5. Measurement & testing

    Testing designs with end-users is arguably the most important part of user-experience and user-centered design. Until I can put a design in front of users, I don't know if it works. Validating workflows with users is an integral part of my design process. Testing includes writing test scripts and moderating task-based analysis where I ask users to think out loud as they work through the test in order to gain insight into their thought process while they use the application. Testing before releasing features to production allows us to make updates for better workflows and designs before users struggle and get frustrated.
